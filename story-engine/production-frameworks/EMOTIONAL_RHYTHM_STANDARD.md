# EMOTIONAL RHYTHM STANDARD
## Bedtalez Arc Architecture & Energy Management
### Authored by Claude Opus | Locked for Sonnet Runtime

---

## THE ARC IS NOT OPTIONAL

Every Bedtalez story follows a precise emotional rhythm arc. This arc is not a suggestion — it is the architecture that makes bedtime storytelling work psychologically.

Do not flatten the arc. Do not maintain constant energy. Do not skip sections.

---

## THE FIVE-STAGE EMOTIONAL ARC

### Stage 1: Opening — Warm + Curious
**Energy target:** Wonder 6 · Calm 8 · Excitement 3

The world appears softly. Something catches the child's attention — a light, a sound, a small mystery. The reader is invited gently in. Nothing happens urgently. The world has weight and texture.

This stage should feel like: stepping into a warm house on a cold evening.

**Duration:** ~12% of story

---

### Stage 2: Adventure — Wonder-Forward
**Energy target:** Wonder 9 · Calm 6 · Excitement 5

The world expands. The child steps toward something. The adventure has a shape, a goal, a quiet pull. Excitement exists but is soft — more like curiosity amplified than adrenaline.

This is the highest-energy section of the story. It does not exceed this energy peak.

**Duration:** ~30% of story

---

### Stage 3: Middle — Gentle Momentum
**Energy target:** Wonder 7 · Calm 7 · Excitement 4

The adventure deepens but does not escalate beyond Stage 2. The child makes meaningful choices. Something small changes — a discovery, a moment of connection, a shift in understanding.

Energy begins its downward journey here. The middle is where momentum becomes tenderness.

**Duration:** ~25% of story

---

### Stage 4: Emotional Moment — Soft + Grounding
**Energy target:** Wonder 5 · Calm 9 · Excitement 2

The heart of the story. The child's internal realisation — the NEED resolved. This section is slower than everything before it.

This is the emotional climax, not a narrative one. Nothing exciting happens here. Something true happens.

**Duration:** ~13% of story

---

### Stage 5: Ending — Calm + Sleepy
**Energy target:** Wonder 4 · Calm 10 · Excitement 1

The world settles. The child returns to safety. Sleep approaches. Every sentence is shorter than the last. Sensory details are minimal and soft. The comfort anchor returns.

This section should feel like: being carried home when very tired, warm and safe.

**Duration:** ~20% of story (longer for sleepy/overtired children)

---

## TRANSITION RULES

Moving between stages requires cinematic transitions — not scene jumps.

**NEVER:** "Then Emma went to the forest."
**ALWAYS:** Carry one atmospheric thread from the current scene into the next — a light, a sound, a texture.

**Example:**
*"As the lantern glow faded behind her, Emma followed the silver path that wound between the sleeping trees."*

The reader must feel the movement, not be told about it.

---

## ENERGY MANAGEMENT RULES

1. Energy never increases after Stage 2
2. Each stage must be detectably calmer than the previous
3. Excitement language (exclamations, sudden events, speed words) is banned after Stage 3
4. The emotional peak (Stage 4) is soft — it is not a crescendo
5. The ending (Stage 5) is the quietest section — if it isn't, the arc has failed

---

## THE BREATHING SPACE

Between Stage 4 and Stage 5, insert 2–3 sentences of absolute stillness.

No action. No dialogue. No new information. Just the world breathing out.

This is non-negotiable. It is the psychological transition point between adventure and sleep-readiness.

---

## SCENE-STAGE CONSISTENCY CHECKS

At each stage transition, verify:
- [ ] Energy has decreased (not maintained, not increased)
- [ ] No new characters or plot elements introduced after Stage 3
- [ ] Sensory language matches the energy target (fewer details as energy decreases)
- [ ] Sentence length trend is downward in Stages 4–5
- [ ] Breathing space exists between Stage 4 and 5

---

*The emotional arc is the story. Without it, you have events. With it, you have experience.*
