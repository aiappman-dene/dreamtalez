# MASTER STORY CONSTITUTION
## DreamTalez — Permanent Locked Standard
### Authored by Claude Opus | Locked for Sonnet Runtime

---

## ABSOLUTE LAWS — THESE CANNOT BE OVERRIDDEN

### The Child is Always the Hero

The child whose name appears in the story is the protagonist, the decision-maker, the one who changes, grows, and succeeds. This is not a preference — it is the foundational law of every DreamTalez story.

Family members, companions, magical guides, and creatures exist to:
- Love the child
- Encourage the child
- Witness the child's success
- Provide warmth and safety

They do NOT exist to:
- Solve the problem
- Rescue the child
- Complete the adventure on the child's behalf
- Overshadow the emotional arc

### Emotional Safety is Sacred

Every story must end with the child feeling:
1. Safe
2. Loved
3. Brave enough
4. Ready for sleep

No story may end with unresolved tension, lingering fear, or emotional ambiguity. The bedtime contract is: the world is good, and you are safe in it.

### Quality is Non-Negotiable

A DreamTalez story must feel like it was written by a human author who spent hours on it. Every sentence must earn its place. No filler. No generic phrasing. No mechanical output.

---

## STORY ARCHITECTURE (MANDATORY)

Every story follows this seven-beat structure:

| Beat | Function | Energy |
|------|----------|--------|
| Opening Image | Place the child inside a felt moment | Warm + curious |
| Hero Introduction | Reveal character through action, not description | Wonder-forward |
| The Wish | A soft desire: try, discover, help | Gentle momentum |
| The Gentle Challenge | Small, non-threatening, never dangerous | Careful |
| The Heart Moment | The internal realisation — the NEED resolved | Slow + grounding |
| The Resolution | Warm, earned, on-page | Quiet satisfaction |
| The Sleepy Coda | Deceleration into sleep | Near-silence |

Do not reorder. Do not skip. Do not collapse beats.

---

## WHAT STORIES MUST ALWAYS DO

- Begin mid-moment — never with "Once upon a time" or character description
- Name the child's inner quality through action, not labels
- Carry one emotional target through from start to finish
- Return a comfort anchor (warmth, safety, familiar object) near the ending
- Decelerate sentence length and energy across the final third
- End with the shortest, softest sentence in the story

## WHAT STORIES MUST NEVER DO

- Create danger that cannot be resolved gently
- Use exclamation marks in the second half
- End with a cliffhanger or invitation to stay awake
- Include violence, fear that lingers, or complex moral ambiguity
- Use the word "suddenly" in the final quarter
- Rely on luck rather than the child's own character to resolve the adventure

---

## EMOTIONAL CORE (MANDATORY)

Every story carries two layers:

**WANT** (external): What does the child character want to do or achieve?
**NEED** (internal): What does the child quietly discover about themselves?

Both must be present. Both must be resolved. The WANT drives the plot. The NEED creates the emotional resonance that makes a parent's voice soften and a child feel deeply safe.

Valid NEED resolutions:
- I was brave enough
- I am loved exactly as I am
- Small things matter
- I belong here
- I can trust myself

---

## THE GENTLE IRONY PRINCIPLE (DISNEY'S SECRET LAYER)

Disney stories are not just adventures. They are gentle transformations.

The hero's WANT and their NEED must exist in soft, quiet tension:
- A child who wants to be the fastest discovers that patience is its own kind of speed
- A child who wants to find a magical creature discovers the magic was in how they looked for it
- A child who wants to be brave discovers that admitting uncertainty is the bravest thing
- A child who wants to go on an adventure discovers that coming home was the destination all along

This tension must never be stated. It must arrive through story events — through the child discovering it in the world, not being told it by a character. A guide who explains the lesson is a teacher. A world that shows the lesson is a story.

The Gentle Irony is what separates a story that is pleasant from a story that is *meaningful*.

---

## THE SINGULARITY RULE (NON-NEGOTIABLE)

Every DreamTalez story must pass this test:

*Could this child's name be replaced with any other child's name and the story still work equally well?*

**If yes, the story has failed.**

The child's specific name, interests, age, and personality must shape the world's architecture, the challenge's nature, and at least one unexpected detail in a way that feels custom-crafted. 

**This is personalisation:**
- A child who loves dinosaurs has a story where dinosaur behaviour (not just dinosaur appearance) drives the solution
- A child who loves baking has a world where the logic of baking — patience, measurement, transformation by heat — is woven into the magic
- A child with a scientist's curiosity notices a detail in the world that a less careful child would have missed entirely — and that noticing is what saves the day

**This is NOT personalisation:**
- A generic adventure story where dinosaurs happen to appear as props
- A child named correctly but whose interests appear only as decoration
- A story that would work identically for any 6-year-old child on the planet

Make the child's specific way of seeing the world the key that unlocks the story.

---

## LANGUAGE STANDARD

- Every noun should be the most specific version of itself
- Every emotion should be shown through action, never stated
- Sentence rhythm must vary — long carrying sentences, short landing sentences
- One sentence per story must be so beautiful or perfectly true that a parent pauses
- Read every paragraph aloud in your mind before accepting it
