# CINEMATIC PROSE STANDARD
## Bedtalez Scene Construction & Transition System
### Authored by Claude Opus | Locked for Sonnet Runtime

---

## CINEMATIC PRINCIPLE

A Bedtalez story is read aloud by a parent in a dark bedroom. It must work as a performance — every sentence must carry sound, texture, and feeling. Read every paragraph as if you are the parent, speaking softly to a child whose eyes are growing heavy.

If a sentence doesn't work spoken aloud slowly and warmly, it doesn't belong in the story.

---

## THE SENSORY OPENING

Every story begins inside a felt moment — not with scene-setting, not with character introduction, not with backstory.

**Wrong openers:**
- "Once upon a time, there was a girl named Emma..."
- "Emma was six years old and loved butterflies..."
- "In a land far away..."
- "It was a dark and stormy night..."

**Right openers** — begin mid-breath, already inside the world:
- "The butterfly net had been hanging on Emma's door all summer, waiting."
- "Something glowed at the bottom of the garden. Just once. Then again."
- "The map had been folded into her pocket so many times that the crease lines felt like rivers."

The first sentence must make a parent lean forward.

---

## SCENE CONSTRUCTION

Every scene must contain:
1. **An anchor** — where are we? (one specific sensory detail)
2. **A presence** — who or what is here?
3. **A feeling** — what emotional temperature does this scene carry?
4. **A direction** — what is moving, changing, or building?

Scenes that contain none of these are filler. Remove them.

---

## CINEMATIC TRANSITION SYSTEM

Transitions between scenes must carry an atmospheric thread. One element — a light source, a sound, a sensation — travels from the end of one scene into the beginning of the next.

**The thread rule:** never let the reader fall between scenes into empty space.

**Examples:**

*Thread: warm light*
"...the lantern swayed gently in the doorway behind her."
→ "The same warm glow seemed to follow her into the forest, settling in the spaces between the trees."

*Thread: sound*
"...and the wind chime above the gate made its small, silver sound."
→ "That same small sound seemed to find her even here, deep in the meadow."

*Thread: texture*
"...the moss was soft under her feet, like the carpet at home."
→ "The moss softened still further as she went deeper, until it felt like she was walking on clouds."

---

## SENSORY ORCHESTRATION

Strong sensory detail belongs at emotional moments — not throughout the story.

**Reserve for:**
1. The moment of greatest wonder (Stage 2 peak)
2. The emotional turning point (Stage 4)
3. The final calming beat (Stage 5 ending)

One well-placed sensory cue creates ten times the immersion of five scattered ones.

**Sensory palette by emotional moment:**

| Moment | Sensory Cue |
|--------|------------|
| Wonder peak | Silver stars shimmering overhead; light fracturing through leaves |
| Comfort | Warm blanket texture; smell of home; fireplace warmth |
| Emotional moment | The way the light went very still; a sound that stopped |
| Safety | The soft weight of familiar things; the warmth of being held |
| Sleep-readiness | A quiet lantern glow; rain on windows; the hush of settled things |

---

## DIALOGUE PRINCIPLES

Dialogue is used sparingly — it is punctuation in Bedtalez prose, not the main event.

Rules:
- Maximum 5–7 dialogue exchanges in a full story
- Each line of dialogue must carry either character or emotion — never both at once
- Dialogue in the final third should be short and warm
- No dialogue in the final paragraph
- Companion/guide dialogue is warmer and shorter than the child's

---

## THE ONE ELEGANT LINE

Every story must contain one sentence so precise and true that a parent reading aloud will slow down without realising it. This line belongs near the Heart Moment.

It is not decorative. It is not flowery. It is the truest thing in the story.

**Examples:**
- "She had been afraid the whole time, and she had done it anyway — and it turned out those two things could be true at once."
- "The fox looked at her the way her grandmother looked at her — as if she were the answer to something."
- "It was a very small brave thing. But small brave things are the ones that matter most."

## PARAGRAPH RHYTHM

| Story Section | Paragraph Pattern |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Opening | Medium → Short → Medium (invitation rhythm) |
| Adventure | Long → Medium → Short (forward momentum) |
| Heart Moment | Short → Very short → Short (emotional landing) |
| Ending | Short → Shorter → Shortest → [silence] |

---

## ENDING ORCHESTRATION

The final scene must contain all of the following:

1. No new information — only warmth and resolution
2. Return of the comfort anchor (familiar light, warmth, or object)
3. Night world settling (stars, moonlight, rain, quiet)
4. Physical comfort signal (blanket, bed, warmth)
5. The shortest sentence in the story — the final one

**Never:**
- "The End."
- A cliffhanger
- An invitation to imagine more
- A rushed resolution
- A sudden new detail

**Always:**
- A feeling of earned completion
- The sense that the world is good and safe
- A final sentence that could be a lullaby line
